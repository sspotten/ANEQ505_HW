Paper discussion: Minaya et al. 2024, “Transfer of microbiota from lean donors in combination with prebiotics prevents excessive weight gain and improves gut-brain vagal signaling in obese rats”

# Figure 5c, d discussion:

**
These two figures show how the rats in the different microbiome treatment groups differed in their motivation and ability to learn. They show the time to acquisition of FR5 criteria between the different treatment groups (FR5 criteria being that the rats had learned to press the lever 5 times to receive a food reward, and repeated this learned behavior five consecutive times within 20 minutes).

Figure C shows the data between the two pre-inoculation treatment groups: the rats fed low-fat chow and the rats fed high-fat chow. It shows the time in seconds that it took each group to reach FR5 criteria when trained on the food-reward task. As you can see, the mean time to FR5 for the HF rats was higher, at about 300 seconds, than the mean time to FR5 for the LF rats, about 250 seconds. This difference was found to be statistically significant.

Figure D shows the time to FR5 for the four post-inoculation treatment groups: the control LF rats, LF-LF rats, HF-LF rats, and HF-HF rats. The high-fat chow-eating rats inoculated with the microbiota from high-fat-fed rats had a statistically significantly higher time to acquire FR5 criteria than the other three groups at about 400 seconds (which, incidentally, is also higher than the pre-inoculation time for high-fat-fed rats). Even though the HF-LF rats were still eating their high-fat chow, being inoculated with microbiota from low-fat-diet-acclimated rats allowed them to gain the same cognitive and motivational benefits as the other two groups eating low-fat chow, and their task acquisition time decreased after inoculation to match that of the low-fat-chow-eating groups.
